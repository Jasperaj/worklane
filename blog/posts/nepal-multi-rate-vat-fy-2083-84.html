Nepal's Multi-Rate VAT is Here (FY 2083/84): 5% on Ride-Sharing and Electricity — What Businesses Must Do

By Upaj Manandhar, Chartered Accountant Jul 17, 2026 11 min read

For nearly three decades, Nepal has run one of the simplest VAT regimes in South Asia: a single 13% rate on almost everything taxable, with a short list of exemptions. The Finance Act 2083 quietly ends that era. Section 55 of the VAT Act, 2052 has been amended to let the Government of Nepal prescribe different VAT rates for specified goods or services through a Nepal Gazette notice, capped at 13%. The government has already used that power to introduce 5% VAT on ride-sharing services and 5% VAT on electricity supplied to final consumers, both effective from Shrawan 1, 2083 (17 July 2026). This is a structural change, not a rate tweak. Here is what it means, and what businesses need to do this week.

Key takeaways

  • Nepal now has a multi-rate VAT regime. The standard rate is still 13%; lower rates (currently 5%) can be prescribed by gazette notice.
  • Ride-sharing platforms (Pathao, InDrive, Indriver, Yatri and similar) must collect 5% VAT on vehicle and delivery services from Shrawan 1, 2083 (17 July 2026).
  • Electricity to final consumers attracts 5% VAT from the same date. Households consuming up to 50 units per month remain exempt; inter-business (B2B) electricity supply stays outside the levy.
  • The gazette-rate framework itself came into force 29 May 2026 — expect more categories to migrate to lower rates in the coming months.
  • Businesses must update billing systems, tax invoices, and VAT-return categorisation before the first Shrawan 2083 invoice goes out.

The big shift — from single rate to multi-rate

The original VAT Act, 2052 was written around a clean two-outcome model: a supply is either taxable at 13% or it sits in one of the three tightly-defined baskets (zero-rated, exempt, or outside scope). This kept the compliance overhead low — no rate-mapping tables, no HSN-driven rate lookups, no "which slab does my restaurant meal fall into" arguments.

The Finance Act 2083 amends Section 55 to add a new sub-section giving the Government of Nepal power to prescribe different VAT rates for specified goods or services through a Nepal Gazette notice, provided the rate does not exceed the standard 13%. The government has immediately used that power in two directions:

  • Ride-sharing platform services at 5%
  • Electricity to final consumers at 5%

What was once a policy that needed a full Finance Act amendment is now a policy that needs a gazette notification. Expect the pace of change to increase.

5% VAT on ride-sharing platforms

The amendment targets resident ride-sharing platform operators — companies that operate an electronic platform on which independent drivers or delivery partners offer transport or delivery services to end-users. In practice, the affected players in Nepal include Pathao, InDrive, Indriver, Yatri and similar operators, whether app-based or web-based.

What the platform must do

  • Register or update its VAT registration to reflect the platform-service classification.
  • Calculate 5% VAT on the taxable value of every vehicle or delivery service booked through the platform — irrespective of whether the driver / delivery partner is individually VAT-registered.
  • Collect the VAT at the point of the customer's payment (the platform is the collector, not the driver).
  • Deposit and file under the standard monthly VAT-return cycle within 25 days of the Nepali month-end (see our VAT return filing guide).
  • Update tax invoices and rider receipts to show the 5% VAT line separately.

What it means for drivers and partners

The individual driver or delivery partner does not directly collect VAT under this regime — the platform does. However, drivers should still keep clean records of platform-issued statements, because their share of the fare (net of commission and VAT) remains their business income subject to Income Tax Act, 2058.

What it means for passengers

Expect a small explicit "VAT 5%" line on your Pathao or InDrive receipt from Shrawan 1. Whether that lands as a fare increase, a commission absorption, or something in between will depend on each platform's pricing strategy.

5% VAT on electricity to final consumers

Electricity to final consumers — meaning residential and small commercial end-users — now carries 5% VAT. The design has two important carve-outs:

  • Lifeline exemption: Households consuming up to 50 units per month remain exempt from VAT. This preserves affordability for the lowest-consumption users.
  • Inter-business (B2B) electricity sales — for example, IPP-to-NEA wheeling, industrial power supply arrangements — remain outside the VAT levy under the current gazette design.

The Nepal Electricity Authority (NEA) and other licensed distributors will need to update their billing systems to distinguish consumer categories (exempt lifeline / 5% VAT / B2B out-of-scope) and reflect the correct classification on every consumer bill. This is a non-trivial IT project on a compressed timeline.

The lifeline exemption is the more interesting policy signal than the 5% rate itself — Nepal is willing to build category-specific VAT thresholds inside a lower rate, not just at the standard 13%.

The gazette power — what to watch next

Because the framework itself is now gazette-driven, further categories can be added without waiting for the next Finance Act. Sectors that have been the subject of long-running rate debates — education services, healthcare, digital/OTT services, essential food items, transport of goods — are the obvious candidates for future notifications. We suggest treating any communication from the IRD about your sector in FY 2083/84 as potentially rate-relevant.

Two structural observations worth keeping in mind:

  • Cap at 13%. The gazette power cannot raise the rate above the standard — it can only cut. This is a one-way street toward more granularity, not toward higher tax burden per se.
  • Rates below 13% carry the input-credit apportionment risk. A supplier selling both 13% and 5% outputs may need to apportion input credit — a compliance step the industry has largely avoided until now.

Input credit under a multi-rate regime

The input-credit rules in Section 17 of the VAT Act continue to apply. In simple terms:

  • Purchases used wholly to make taxable outputs (whether at 5%, 13% or 0%) attract full input credit.
  • Purchases used wholly to make exempt supplies attract no input credit.
  • Purchases used for a mix require apportionment in the ratio of taxable to total supplies.

The important consequence: a ride-sharing platform charging 5% output can still claim input credit at 13% on IT infrastructure, office rent (where applicable), professional fees and other inputs. On the numbers, this can create net refund positions in early quarters — plan cashflow accordingly.

Frequently asked questions

Does 5% VAT apply to freelance drivers I book directly (not via a platform)?

No. The 5% rate is specifically tied to the platform-service classification. A direct hire of a driver or vehicle, outside a covered electronic platform, falls under the general Section 88 framework (typically 10% TDS on hire of vehicle, and 13% VAT if the operator is VAT-registered).

What about food delivery (Foodmandu, Pathao Food)?

The gazette wording captures "vehicle or delivery services provided through the platform," which reads to include food and parcel delivery in scope of the 5% rate. Practitioners should watch for a follow-up IRD clarification specific to food-aggregator platforms, since the underlying restaurant supply already carries its own VAT treatment.

Is 5% VAT on electricity bill collected by NEA a "final tax"?

No. It is a standard VAT charge — NEA is the collector, the treasury is the beneficiary, and the consumer's bill is the tax invoice. If the consumer is a VAT-registered business using the connection for taxable business, they can claim input credit as usual.

Does the 50-unit lifeline exemption apply per bill or per meter?

Per meter, per billing cycle — matching NEA's existing tariff design. Where a household has multiple meters, each meter's monthly consumption is measured separately.

How should I show the 5% rate on my Tally Prime invoice?

Create a new VAT ledger under Duties & Taxes with rate 5% and type "VAT," link it to the affected sales ledgers, and ensure the printed invoice format shows the rate breakdown line. Our Tally automation guide covers deeper workflow automation.

Getting your VAT setup ready

The move to multi-rate VAT is small in size and large in consequence — every VAT-registered business now needs to think about rate classification, mixed-supply apportionment and gazette-monitoring in a way that was optional before. If your accounting stack still assumes one VAT rate, this week is the time to fix it.
