Types of AI Tools 2026: Which One to Use for What (Complete Guide)

By Upaj Manandhar, Chartered Accountant Jul 7, 2026 · Updated Jul 15, 2026 11 min read

"AI" now covers a dozen genuinely different kinds of tools. In 2026, the practical challenge for professionals is no longer whether to use AI — it is which AI to use for which job. Choosing the right one matters far more than choosing the "best" one. This guide is a working map of the modern AI landscape, organised by what you are actually trying to accomplish.

Key takeaways

  • General chat models (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot) are excellent at drafting and reasoning, but they can hallucinate — treat them as junior colleagues, not oracles.
  • For anything where accuracy matters, use a source-grounded tool (NotebookLM, Perplexity) that cites where each claim came from.
  • Coding and workflow agents (Claude Code, Cursor, GitHub Copilot) increasingly complete multi-step tasks end-to-end — including in finance and operations, not just software.
  • Meeting AI (Otter, Fireflies, Zoom/Teams AI Companion) is the quietest productivity win — most offices have not turned it on.
  • Confidentiality lives in the vendor's data-handling terms, not the model. Read them before pasting client information.

The AI toolset in 2026 — categories by primary use

Match the tool to the task. Most Nepali professionals end up with two or three tools across categories, not one "best" tool.

1. General chat assistants — the everyday workhorse

Anthropic Claude, OpenAI ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Microsoft Copilot. These are the tools most people think of when they hear "AI." They can draft, summarise, explain, analyse, translate, brainstorm and — with a few caveats — reason. For professionals they are best treated as a tireless junior colleague: excellent first drafts of letters, policies, analyses and emails, always reviewed by you.

Each has its own personality and strong suits:

  • Claude (Anthropic) — notably strong on long documents (200K+ tokens of context), careful reasoning, and following complex instructions. Good default when you need a considered answer.
  • ChatGPT (OpenAI) — the broadest ecosystem of GPTs, plugins and integrations; strong multimodal features (voice, vision, image generation via DALL·E baked in).
  • Gemini (Google) — ties tightly into Google Workspace (Docs, Sheets, Gmail, Meet). Natural choice if your organisation is Google-first.
  • Copilot (Microsoft) — lives inside Microsoft 365 (Word, Excel, Outlook, Teams). Natural choice if your organisation is Microsoft-first.

All four are competitive on general tasks; the tie-breaker is usually the workflow around the model — where your data lives, which suite your team already uses, and what enterprise data-privacy tier you can afford.

2. Research and grounding tools — for anything fact-sensitive

Perplexity, Google NotebookLM, deep-research modes. These tools cite sources and either search the live web (Perplexity, ChatGPT Search, Gemini Deep Research) or answer strictly from documents you upload (NotebookLM). Because their outputs are linked to evidence, they hallucinate far less — and when they do, you can catch it immediately.

  • Perplexity — the reference tool for "what is happening with X?" questions where you need live sources.
  • NotebookLM — upload PDFs, Docs, slides, websites, YouTube URLs and audio, then ask questions with page-level citations. Its Audio Overview feature turns a document set into a podcast-style discussion. (We wrote about NotebookLM specifically in this post.)
  • Deep Research modes in ChatGPT, Gemini and Claude produce longer, referenced reports over 10–30 minutes of autonomous browsing — appropriate for market scans, regulation summaries and literature reviews.
If someone will act on the answer — a client, a regulator, a court — use a tool that cites its sources.

3. Coding and agentic tools — for tasks that need to do things

Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Windsurf. These tools write and refactor code, and increasingly run multi-step tasks on their own (this is what "agentic" means): reading files, calling other tools, running commands and completing a workflow end-to-end. Finance and operations teams — not just engineering — now use them for:

  • Automating recurring report generation from CSV/Excel dumps.
  • Cleaning and reconciling large data sets that Excel would struggle with.
  • Building small internal utilities (PDF extractors, ledger validators, form-filler scripts) without a full engineering project.

The productivity delta here is large. A finance analyst who can describe a problem clearly can now have a working script by lunchtime instead of a change-request in a queue for three weeks.

4. Image, video and audio generators — for creative work

Midjourney, DALL·E, Google Imagen, Runway, Sora, Veo, ElevenLabs, Suno. These generate visuals, video and voice from prompts. For businesses the honest use case is drafts: marketing visuals, social imagery, explainer-video scenes, voiceovers for training material.

  • Midjourney / DALL·E / Imagen — still imagery.
  • Runway / Sora / Veo — short video clips from text or images.
  • ElevenLabs / Suno — realistic voice synthesis and short music tracks.

Two cautions: (a) treat outputs as first drafts requiring brand and rights review, and (b) do not use client photographs or logos as inputs without checking IP and consent — some tools reserve rights over inputs and outputs in their free tiers.

5. Meeting and workflow AI — the underused productivity layer

Otter.ai, Fireflies, tl;dv, Microsoft Teams AI Companion, Zoom AI Companion, Google Meet's built-in Take Notes. These join your meetings, produce accurate transcripts, summaries and action items, and make every past discussion searchable.

The quiet productivity win most offices skip: instead of scattered handwritten notes, you get an accurate, searchable record of every client call — instantly usable for follow-ups, brief hand-offs, and later fact-checks. A prospective client mentioned a specific deadline two months ago? Search the transcript.

6. Specialised professional AI

Beyond the general categories, entire product areas are being rebuilt around AI. A few relevant to professional-services teams:

  • Document AI (e.g. Adobe AI Assistant, Humata, ChatPDF) — question-and-answer over a specific PDF or contract.
  • Legal AI (Harvey, Spellbook) — contract review, clause libraries, argument drafting.
  • Data / analytics AI (Julius, Copilot in Excel) — statistical analysis and charting from a natural-language prompt over your spreadsheet.
  • Customer AI (Intercom Fin, Zendesk AI Agents) — 24/7 first-line support drawing on your help-centre content.

Choosing well — a short decision framework

  • Facts need grounding. If someone will act on the answer, use a research tool that cites its sources, not a bare chat model.
  • Confidential data needs policy, not hope. Check the tool's data-handling terms before pasting client information. Enterprise tiers of Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini and Copilot generally exclude your inputs from training; free tiers usually do not.
  • Repetitive work suits agents; judgement stays human. Automate what is boring, review what is important, sign nothing you have not read.
  • Start narrow. One tool, one workflow, one month. Measure the time you actually save, then expand what works.
The gap is no longer between people who use AI and people who don't — it is between people who match the tool to the task and people who don't.

Frequently asked questions

Which AI is best for accountants, auditors and tax professionals?

For long-document reasoning (an Act, a directive, an audit file), Claude and NotebookLM lead. For Excel-native work, Copilot in Microsoft 365. For sourced research on rulings and news, Perplexity. Most firms end up using two or three in combination.

Do these tools work well with Nepali content?

Yes — Claude, ChatGPT and Gemini all handle Nepali (Devanagari) text competently for reading, summarising, translating and drafting. Quality is best in English but usable in Nepali. For anything client-facing, review the Nepali output line by line.

How much does this cost?

Consumer tiers of most tools are free with limits. Paid individual plans are broadly USD 20/month per tool. Enterprise tiers vary; if you use more than one tool heavily, an AI Gateway subscription (e.g. Poe, T3 Chat) that bundles access to multiple models can be cheaper.

Should we build one custom AI tool for our firm?

Usually not — first. Get value from off-the-shelf tools for six months, understand where the real bottlenecks are, then decide whether a custom tool solves a problem no vendor does. Most "we need a custom AI" projects turn out to be "we need a well-configured NotebookLM."
