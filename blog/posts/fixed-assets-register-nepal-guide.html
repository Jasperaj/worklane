Fixed Assets Register in Nepal: Build It From Scratch, Maintain It, Track It

By Upaj Manandhar, Chartered Accountant Jul 17, 2026 14 min read

The Fixed Assets Register (FAR) is the accounting record most Nepali SMEs postpone building until an audit forces them to. That is a mistake — the FAR is not a bureaucratic form; it is the operating record that connects a physical thing in your office to a number on your balance sheet, and it drives depreciation, insurance, disposal accounting, tax positions and internal control at the same time. This guide walks through building a FAR from scratch, running it properly month after month, and keeping physical reality and the ledger reconciled — with a copy-ready Excel schema, worked examples, and Nepal-specific depreciation under Schedule 2 of the Income Tax Act 2058.

Key takeaways

  • A FAR is required by both the Income Tax Act 2058 (Schedule 2) for block-wise WDV depreciation and by NFRS/NAS 16 for financial-statement disclosures — you cannot get either right without it.
  • Every asset needs a unique asset code, a physical tag, and a row in the register — no exceptions. If it does not tie back, it does not exist.
  • Nepal's tax depreciation uses five blocks (A–E) with WDV rates 5% / 25% / 20% / 15% / 10% respectively, and a 1/3rd rule for mid-year additions.
  • Book depreciation (NAS 16 straight-line) and tax depreciation (Schedule 2 WDV) will not match — that is normal, and creates deferred tax.
  • Physical verification once a year is the minimum; rolling verification of high-value or mobile assets each quarter is the standard we recommend.

Why the FAR matters

A FAR is doing four jobs at the same time:

  • Substantiating your depreciation charge — under the Income Tax Act 2058 (Sections 19 and Schedule 2), depreciation is a deductible business expense only if you can show which pool the asset sits in and when it was added.
  • Underpinning NFRS/NAS 16 disclosures — cost, accumulated depreciation, additions, disposals and impairment must be reconciled at the level of the asset class.
  • Enabling internal control — knowing which laptop is with which employee, where the projector actually lives, and whether the generator serial number matches the invoice.
  • Enabling insurance and disposal decisions — you cannot insure or scrap an asset you cannot find.

Asset coding and tagging

A good code makes the register self-documenting. A workable structure for a small Nepali business:

WL-KTM-B-014 — where WL is your entity, KTM is the location, B is the tax block, 014 is a running serial.

  • Print the code on a durable label (thermal / laminated) and stick it in a fixed, visible location on the asset.
  • For high-mobility assets (laptops, cameras), use a QR sticker that resolves to the FAR row when scanned.
  • Never reuse a retired code — extend the serial forever.
  • Photograph every asset at the time of tagging; keep the photo in a shared drive folder named after the code.

Depreciation — Schedule 2 blocks A–E

Under Schedule 2 of the Income Tax Act 2058, assets are grouped into five pools (blocks), each with its own WDV rate. The block, not the individual asset, is what depreciates:

Nepal tax depreciation rates (WDV, per block)

Source: Income Tax Act 2058, Schedule 2. Block E depreciation is amortised over the useful life of the intangible.

Every fixed asset must be assigned to exactly one block. If you cannot decide between two blocks for a particular asset — say, a specialised piece of testing equipment — the safer position is usually the higher-rate block, but confirm with your auditor.

The 1/3rd rule for mid-year additions

Additions to a block during the fiscal year do not get a full year of depreciation. Schedule 2 uses a three-tier rule based on when in the year the asset was put to use:

  • First 4 months (Shrawan–Kartik): depreciation on the full addition amount.
  • Middle 4 months (Mangsir–Falgun): depreciation on 2/3rd of the addition.
  • Last 4 months (Chaitra–Ashadh): depreciation on 1/3rd of the addition.

This is a Nepal-specific convention — do not import the Indian half-year rule or the international mid-month convention. It matters because most acquisitions land near the fiscal-year end (Chaitra–Ashadh), and getting the fraction wrong understates or overstates deductible depreciation.

Worked example — a Kathmandu SME's first year

Suppose a consulting firm buys the following assets during FY 2082/83:

AssetCost (NPR)BlockDate put to useDepreciable amount
Office fit-out15,00,000A (5%)Shrawan (early)Full (15,00,000)
Laptops × 57,50,000B (25%)Bhadra (early)Full (7,50,000)
Server4,00,000B (25%)Mangsir (mid)2/3 = 2,66,667
Delivery van25,00,000C (20%)Baisakh (late)1/3 = 8,33,333

The FY 2082/83 tax depreciation is:

  • Block A: 15,00,000 × 5% = NPR 75,000
  • Block B: (7,50,000 + 2,66,667) × 25% = NPR 2,54,167
  • Block C: 8,33,333 × 20% = NPR 1,66,667
  • Total tax depreciation FY 2082/83: NPR 4,95,834
The 1/3rd rule is not a rounding convention — it is a real reduction in your first-year deductible depreciation. Plan capex acquisitions in the first four months of the year where you can.

Book vs tax depreciation — deferred tax

Book depreciation under NAS 16 is typically Straight Line over estimated useful life (e.g., laptops 3 years, servers 5 years, buildings 30-40 years, vehicles 5-7 years). Tax depreciation under Schedule 2 is WDV pool-based. These will almost never match. The difference is a timing difference giving rise to deferred tax under NAS 12.

Monthly maintenance workflow

  1. Additions: Every asset invoice through the AP cycle should generate a FAR row before it hits the general ledger. Never post to Fixed Assets without a code being created.
  2. Transfers: If a laptop moves from one employee to another, update the Custodian column and log the change.
  3. WIP/CWIP: Track assets under construction separately; capitalise (move to FAR proper) on the date the asset is put to use, not on the date of the final invoice.
  4. Book depreciation: Run automatically monthly through your accounting software or an Excel formula linked to the FAR.
  5. Reconciliation: At month-end, tie the FAR closing balance to the ledger (Fixed Assets − Accumulated Depreciation). A recurring 1% mismatch is a broken FAR.
  6. Insurance: Renew insurance schedules against the FAR — undervalued cover is a common gap.

Physical verification — how, and how often

  • Annual full verification at year-end (Ashadh) is the minimum. Sign-off must come from a person independent of the custodian.
  • Quarterly rolling verification of high-value or mobile assets (laptops, cameras, vehicles). Rotate the sample so every asset is verified at least twice a year.
  • Location-based sweeps: One team, one floor, one afternoon. Print a location-filtered FAR extract and walk with it.
  • Discrepancy handling:
    • Found but not in FAR → investigate; if genuine, back-date the capitalisation.
    • In FAR but not found → search 30 days, ask custodian, escalate; only then write off with management approval.
    • Wrong location/custodian → simple update, log the change.

Disposals, transfers and impairment

  • Sale: Book proceeds; retire the asset in the FAR; compute book gain/loss (proceeds − net book value) and tax impact (proceeds reduce block WDV; if proceeds exceed block WDV, the excess is taxable gain).
  • Scrap / write-off: Requires management approval and, ideally, a physical destruction / scrap note.
  • Transfer between locations: No accounting entry; update Location and Custodian in the FAR.
  • Impairment: NAS 36 requires review of assets showing indicators of impairment (obsolete, damaged, market value collapse). Book an impairment loss; tax deduction may be available only on actual disposal.

Handy Excel formulas for the register:

  • 1/3rd fraction based on month of use:
    =IF(MONTH(PutToUse)<=4,1, IF(MONTH(PutToUse)<=8,2/3, 1/3))

    =(Cost - Salvage) / Life

    =(TaxOpWDV + TaxAdd_Full + TaxAdd_TwoThird*2/3 + TaxAdd_OneThird/3) * BlockRate

Frequently asked questions

Can I keep the FAR only in Tally Prime?

Tally's built-in Fixed Assets module handles the ledger side, but it does not natively track custodian, location, or verification history. Most Nepali businesses keep Tally for the accounting and Excel/ERP for the operational FAR, reconciled monthly.

What about assets below a de-minimis (NPR 5,000, 10,000)?

Many businesses have a policy to expense assets below a threshold (say NPR 5,000 per item). This is an accounting policy choice and must be applied consistently. For tax, Schedule 2 does not exempt low-value assets — technically they still enter the block; in practice, immaterial items are expensed.

Can VAT be capitalised into asset cost?

Only if you cannot claim input credit on it. VAT-registered businesses that use the asset in taxable supplies exclude VAT from the capitalised cost.

How do I handle a fully-depreciated asset that is still in use?

Keep it in the FAR at Rs 1 or its salvage value, with status "In Use." Retire only on physical disposal. It is not unusual for well-run FARs to carry 10-year-old items at nominal value simply for control.

What if my company file has never had a proper FAR?

Do a "day-zero" physical count, build the register bottom-up, back-solve depreciation from purchase invoices where available, and open the register with a management-approved reconciliation to the ledger. It is uncomfortable but one-time; done properly, you are set for a decade.

Getting your FAR set up correctly

Building a FAR from scratch usually takes 3–5 working days for a small business — most of it physical counting and back-solving purchase history. Doing it once, properly, saves you every year at audit.
