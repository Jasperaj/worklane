25 Excel Formulas Every Finance Professional Must Know in 2026 (With Examples)

By Upaj Manandhar, Chartered Accountant Jul 8, 2026 · Updated Jul 15, 2026 14 min read

Excel remains the working language of finance. The difference between someone who "uses Excel" and someone who closes the books two days faster is usually a short list of formulas — used well, combined in twos and threes, and named clearly. Here are the twenty-five formulas we consider non-negotiable for anyone working in accounting, audit, tax or FP&A, each with a real finance use case and a copy-ready example.

Key takeaways

  • Modern spill functions (XLOOKUP, FILTER, UNIQUE, SORT) remove entire classes of drag-fill errors — prefer them when your Excel version supports them.
  • Formulas rarely work alone. Learn them in pairs: XLOOKUP + IFERROR, SUMIFS + EOMONTH, FILTER + UNIQUE, LET + LAMBDA.
  • Use named ranges and LET to make formulas readable — a finance sheet should be auditable like a working paper.
  • Availability matters: XLOOKUP, FILTER, LET, LAMBDA require Microsoft 365 or Excel 2021+. On older Excel, fall back to INDEX/MATCH and array formulas.

The 25 formulas — by category

Categorised for the FY 2082/83 finance workflow. Modern dynamic-array functions now hold the largest share.

The twenty-five formulas — with a real finance use case for each

Formula — what it doesFinance use case and example
1. SUM / SUMIFS
Total a range; SUMIFS totals with multiple conditions.		Month-wise expense totals by department and cost centre from a ledger dump.
=SUMIFS(Amount, Dept, "Finance", Month, "Shrawan")
2. COUNTIFS
Count rows meeting several criteria.		Count overdue invoices per client for a receivables ageing note.
=COUNTIFS(Client, A2, DaysOverdue, ">30")
3. AVERAGEIFS
Conditional average.		Average processing days for approved-only claims.
=AVERAGEIFS(Days, Status, "Approved")
4. XLOOKUP
Modern lookup — any direction, built-in "if not found".		Pull a vendor's PAN or bank account from a master list into the payment sheet.
=XLOOKUP(A2, Vendors[Name], Vendors[PAN], "Not found")
5. VLOOKUP
Classic vertical lookup (still everywhere).		Map ledger codes to account names in legacy templates you inherited.
=VLOOKUP(A2, CodeMap, 2, FALSE)
6. INDEX + MATCH
Flexible lookup that survives column inserts.		Two-way lookup: rate for a given grade (row) and year (column).
=INDEX(Rates, MATCH(Grade,GradeCol,0), MATCH(Year,YearRow,0))
7. IF / IFS
Branching logic.		Flag variances: within budget / review / escalate.
=IFS(V<5%,"OK", V<10%,"Review", TRUE,"Escalate")
8. IFERROR
Trap errors with a clean fallback.		Wrap lookups so missing codes show 'Not mapped' instead of #N/A.
=IFERROR(VLOOKUP(...), "Not mapped")
9. SUMPRODUCT
Multiply-then-sum arrays; conditional math on older Excel.		Weighted average interest rate across a loan portfolio.
=SUMPRODUCT(Balance,Rate)/SUM(Balance)
10. EOMONTH / EDATE
Month-end and month-shift dates.		Auto-compute VAT deposit deadlines (25 days after month end) and EMI schedules.
=EOMONTH(A2,0)+25
11. TEXT
Format numbers/dates as text.		Build invoice references like "INV-2082-07" from dates.
="INV-"&TEXT(A2,"yyyy-mm")
12. ROUND / ROUNDDOWN / MROUND
Control decimals explicitly.		Round TDS to the rupee so the ledger reconciles with the bank payment.
=ROUND(Gross*1.5%, 0)
13. PMT
Loan instalment for a rate/term.		Test EMI options before signing a term loan.
=PMT(Rate/12, Months, -Principal)
14. NPV / IRR
Discounted value and return of periodic cash flows.		Appraise a capex proposal or compare two investment options.
=NPV(10%, CashFlows) + InitialInvestment
15. XNPV / XIRR
NPV/IRR with actual dates instead of even periods.		Real return on irregular investor drawdowns and distributions.
=XIRR(CashFlows, Dates)
16. FILTER
Spill rows matching criteria (365 / Excel 2021+).		Live extract of unpaid invoices that updates as data changes.
=FILTER(Invoices, Invoices[Status]="Unpaid")
17. UNIQUE
Distinct values.		De-duplicate a customer list before audit circularisation.
=UNIQUE(A2:A5000)
18. SORT / SORTBY
Ordered spill.		Automatic ranked list of top-10 debtors by outstanding.
=SORT(FILTER(...), 2, -1)
19. TEXTJOIN
Concatenate with a delimiter.		Combine address fields for postal labels or e-billing uploads.
=TEXTJOIN(", ", TRUE, A2:D2)
20. TRIM / CLEAN
Strip stray spaces and non-printable characters.		Fix system exports where 'ABC ' silently fails to match 'ABC'.
=TRIM(CLEAN(A2))
21. LET
Name intermediate steps inside a single formula.		Make a slab-based tax formula readable.
=LET(t, TaxableIncome, IF(t<=500000, t*1%, ...))
22. LAMBDA
Define reusable custom functions in the workbook.		One reusable SLABTAX() function for salary TDS across sheets.
23. TEXTSPLIT / TEXTBEFORE / TEXTAFTER
Modern text parsing (365).		Split "Sharma, Ram | Kathmandu" into columns without helper columns.
=TEXTSPLIT(A2, ",", "|")
24. LOOKUP with wildcards
XLOOKUP and MATCH support * ? wildcards.		Match "PVT LTD" companies where names vary slightly across systems.
=XLOOKUP("*"&A2&"*", List, List, "", 2)
25. PIVOTBY / GROUPBY
Formula-driven pivot tables (M365, 2024+).		Live department × month expense pivot that refreshes automatically.
=PIVOTBY(Dept, Month, Amount, SUM)

How to actually learn them

  • Adopt in pairs. Most real tasks combine two formulas — XLOOKUP + IFERROR, SUMIFS + EOMONTH, FILTER + UNIQUE, LET + LAMBDA. Learn the pairs; don't drill single functions.
  • Rebuild one real report. Take last month's manually-built statement and reconstruct it with formulas only — no hard-coded values, no copy-paste. You will find every weak spot in your working knowledge in half a day.
  • Prefer spill functions where available. FILTER, UNIQUE, SORT, SEQUENCE, PIVOTBY remove the "did I drag the formula down far enough?" class of errors entirely.
  • Name your ranges. =SUMIFS(Amount, Month, ReportMonth) is auditable at a glance; =SUMIFS(C:C, A:A, F1) is not.
  • Use LET aggressively. It is the single biggest readability win of the last five years — nesting IFs inside IFs is an anti-pattern in 2026.
  • Learn Power Query for repeatable data-cleaning tasks. Once a workflow appears twice, it belongs in Power Query, not in a formula chain.
A finance sheet should be readable by the next person like a working paper — formulas are your audit trail.

What about AI in Excel?

Microsoft 365 Copilot inside Excel can now generate PIVOTBY, FILTER and even LAMBDA formulas from a natural-language prompt over your data. Treat it the same way you would a fluent but junior colleague — excellent starting point, always reviewed by you before it hits a filed return. The tools we surveyed in our overview of AI tools apply here too: match the tool to the task, don't paste confidential data into free tiers, and verify anything that will be signed.

Getting your team's templates rebuilt
