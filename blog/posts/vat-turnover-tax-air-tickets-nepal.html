VAT and Turnover Tax on Air Tickets in Nepal (FY 2082/83): The Complete Guide

By Upaj Manandhar, Chartered Accountant Jun 30, 2026 · Updated Jul 15, 2026 13 min read

Few tax changes in Nepal have generated as much operational friction as the taxation of air tickets. The rules sit at the intersection of three regimes: the Value Added Tax Act, 2052, the Income Tax Act's special turnover regime for international transport (Section 70A), and the IATA / BSP ticketing systems that airlines actually use globally. Each was designed on its own logic; together they created — and continue to create — a real compliance headache for airlines, travel agents and corporate buyers. Here is how the pieces fit and where they grind.

Key takeaways

  • An air ticket sold in Nepal can attract two distinct taxes — VAT (13%) and a turnover-based income tax on foreign airlines under Section 70A.
  • Foreign airlines pay income tax at 5% of gross fare for embarkation from Nepal, and 2% for transit — instead of profit-based tax.
  • Domestic air travel is subject to 13% VAT; VAT on international tickets has moved through phased treatments and transitional waivers.
  • IATA/BSP systems are not built to add a Nepal-specific VAT line natively — this is the operational bottleneck.
  • Corporate buyers: VAT on domestic sectors is input-creditable if you are VAT-registered.

Two different taxes on one ticket

An air ticket sold in Nepal can attract:

  1. Value Added Tax (13%) — a consumption tax on the transport service, applicable to domestic air travel and, under successive Finance Acts, extended in phases toward the sale of international tickets in Nepal.
  2. Turnover-based income tax on international carriers under Section 70A: foreign airlines operating in Nepal are taxed on a turnover basis for Nepal-origin transport income — 5% for tickets/cargo embarking from Nepal, and 2% for transit-related revenue — instead of paying tax on Nepal-attributable profit.

Why turnover tax exists for airlines — the design logic

Computing a foreign airline's Nepal-attributable profit is nearly impossible in practice:

  • Costs are global (aircraft leases, crew, fuel hedging, brand, IT).
  • Aircraft cross multiple jurisdictions per flight.
  • Revenue accounting sits abroad, in a currency and system Nepal cannot audit.
  • Transfer-pricing disputes on inter-branch allocations would be endless.

So the Act taxes gross Nepal-originating sales at a low flat rate. It is simple, predictable and avoids transfer-pricing arguments. Other airline income (cargo handling, ground services, office rental income) is taxed on the normal profit basis.

The implementation hassle — why this rollout was so bumpy

  • Global systems don't know Nepali VAT. IATA BSP and airline reservation systems (Amadeus, Sabre, Galileo) have tax boxes configured centrally — adding a Nepal-specific VAT line requires central system changes that airlines couldn't make overnight.
  • Agents caught in the middle. Ticket sales agents faced VAT registration and collection obligations for supplies where the actual carrier was a foreign airline — leaving open who invoices whom, whether the full fare or only the agent's commission is the taxable base, and how to reconcile at month-end.
  • Price competitiveness. Adding 13% to tickets sold in Nepal creates an incentive to purchase the same seat abroad, undermining local agents.
  • Transitional waivers. Successive Finance Acts have offered relief windows for service providers and ticket-sales agents who registered for VAT and paid back-period VAT within a specified window — an implicit acknowledgment that the rollout outpaced industry ability to comply.
  • Interpretation drift. Different IRD offices have historically interpreted "commission on international ticket" differently — pushing agents to seek advance rulings.
A tax that is hard to collect through the systems the industry actually uses will eventually be softened, waived, or restructured. Air-ticket VAT in Nepal is the textbook case.

What each stakeholder should be doing

Travel agencies

  • Confirm current VAT registration status and whether the applicable base is full fare or only commission.
  • Reconcile BSP settlement reports monthly against the VAT sales register.
  • Track transitional waiver windows carefully — a missed cut-off crystallises back liability that was otherwise waivable.
  • Keep a documented rate matrix by segment (domestic / international / codeshare) — auditors will ask.

Foreign airlines

  • Section 70A turnover tax is on Nepal-embarkation revenue at 5% and Nepal-transit at 2% — reconcile Nepal GSA reports monthly and file within timelines.
  • Register the GSA / branch office with the IRD and file returns even in loss-making months — filing is procedural under turnover regime.
  • For non-transport revenue (cargo handling, staff housing, office rental), track separately and file under the normal profit-based regime.

Corporate ticket buyers

  • Check invoices: VAT on domestic sectors is input-creditable if you are VAT-registered and the trip is for business.
  • Ensure the ticket invoice carries your PAN — otherwise input credit is disallowed.
  • For international sectors, VAT treatment on the invoice depends on the sale point and current Finance Act treatment; consult the agent's tax note.
  • Keep boarding passes / e-tickets tied to the invoice — required for input credit verification on tax audit.

What businesses should take from this

  • The rules in this area shift with every Finance Act. Confirm the current year's treatment before structuring anything — do not rely on a prior-year understanding.
  • Where a tax is difficult to enforce through the industry's actual systems, expect waivers, phase-ins and interpretation drift. Build compliance workflows that assume this.
  • Corporate travel programmes benefit from a periodic invoice review — most companies over-recover or under-recover VAT on tickets.

Frequently asked questions

Is VAT charged on international air tickets sold in Nepal?

Treatment has moved through several Finance Acts. As of FY 2082/83, international ticketing in Nepal is subject to VAT on defined bases with transitional relief regimes for agents. Always confirm the current Finance Act treatment before invoicing.

Can travel agents claim input credit on their operating expenses?

Yes — VAT-registered agents claim input credit on operating expenses in the normal way (Section 17 of the VAT Act) proportional to taxable output supplies.

How is the 5% turnover tax on foreign airlines computed?

On the gross fare (net of statutory taxes and airport levies) for tickets originating from Nepal and cargo uplift originating from Nepal, plus 2% on transit revenue attributable to Nepal.
