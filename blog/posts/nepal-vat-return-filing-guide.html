VAT Return Filing in Nepal (FY 2082/83): The Complete Practical Guide for Small Businesses

By Upaj Manandhar, Chartered Accountant Jul 9, 2026 · Updated Jul 15, 2026 12 min read

Value Added Tax is the single most frequent compliance touchpoint a Nepali business has with the Inland Revenue Department — and the one where small, avoidable mistakes accumulate into real penalties. This guide walks through, in plain language, who must register under the VAT Act, 2052 (1996) and the VAT Rules, 2053 as amended by the Finance Act 2082, what the monthly filing cycle actually involves for FY 2082/83, how to file on ird.gov.np, where businesses most often go wrong, and how to fix things when you already have.

Key takeaways

  • Registration is mandatory once turnover crosses NPR 50 lakh (goods) or NPR 30 lakh (services / mixed) — and unconditional for specified sectors.
  • The return cycle is monthly, filed within 25 days of the Nepali month-end. A nil return is still a return.
  • Input credit is available only on purchases with a valid PAN/VAT-registered tax invoice and must be claimed within 12 months.
  • Late deposit attracts 15% p.a. interest plus fees; concealment can escalate to penalties up to 100% of the shortfall.
  • e-filing on ird.gov.np is mandatory for most registered persons; paper filing is now the exception.

Who needs to register

Registration is triggered by either turnover or sector:

  • Turnover-based: annual taxable turnover exceeding NPR 50,00,000 for goods, or NPR 30,00,000 for services (or mixed). Turnover is computed on a rolling twelve-month basis, not just the fiscal year.
  • Sector-based: irrespective of turnover, certain sectors must register — including brick manufacturers, hardware, sanitary, colour lab, health and fitness clubs, restaurants above certain classes, tourism-sector businesses (travel, trekking, rafting agencies), and specified professionals (auditors, engineers, consultants, lawyers, doctors' clinics above threshold).
  • Voluntary registration: businesses below the threshold may voluntarily register — often worthwhile when your customers are VAT-registered and want tax invoices to claim input credit.

The monthly filing cycle — step by step

A VAT-registered business files a return for every Nepali calendar month, within 25 days of the month's end, whether or not there were any transactions. The rhythm is simple but unforgiving:

  1. Issue VAT invoices in the prescribed format for every taxable sale (name and PAN of buyer, invoice number in prescribed sequence, HS code where applicable, taxable value, 13% VAT).
  2. Maintain the sales register (बिक्री खाता) — chronological, reconciling to your invoice book.
  3. Maintain the purchase register (खरिद खाता) with vendor invoices attached; only purchases with a valid tax invoice qualify for input credit.
  4. Compute output VAT − input VAT for the month.
  5. File the return (VAT Form) on ird.gov.np and pay the net VAT before the 25th of the following month.
  6. Retain records for at least six years.

The 25-day rule, mapped to the Nepali calendar

Because the Nepali fiscal calendar (Bikram Sambat) does not align with the Gregorian month, the "25 days after month-end" rule can catch owners who plan by Gregorian dates. As a practical example, Shrawan 2082 ends around 16 August 2025 — the VAT return for that month is due on or before Bhadra 25, 2082 (approx. 10 September 2025). Build the reminder against the BS calendar, not the AD one.

e-filing on ird.gov.np — the process

  1. Log in to ird.gov.np with your business PAN username and password.
  2. Navigate to Taxpayer Portal → VAT → Monthly Return.
  3. Enter total taxable sales, exempt sales, zero-rated (export) sales, imports, taxable purchases, exempt purchases and any adjustments.
  4. The system computes VAT payable / refundable and prepares a voucher.
  5. Pay via authorised banks or IRD's online payment gateway (connectIPS, Fonepay, or the bank's own portal).
  6. Download the filed return and payment receipt for your records — this is the document an assessment officer will ask for first.

Input credit — the rules that decide whether you get it

  • You must hold a valid tax invoice from the vendor bearing their VAT registration and your name and PAN.
  • The purchase must be for use in a taxable business supply. Purely personal expenses do not qualify.
  • Certain categories have restricted credit: private automobiles, entertainment expenses, alcohol, and some construction materials often carry input credit restrictions — check current directives.
  • Input credit must be claimed within 12 months of the invoice date.
  • Where taxable and exempt supplies are mixed, input credit is apportioned in the ratio of taxable to total supplies.

Where businesses go wrong — the top eight mistakes

  1. Missing nil returns during quiet months — a nil return is still due, and non-filing attracts penalties.
  2. Claiming input credit on purchases without a proper tax invoice — a bhat-pai chit is not enough.
  3. Sales and purchase registers that don't reconcile with the filed return figures — the first thing an assessment officer checks.
  4. Late registration after the threshold was crossed months earlier — back-dating brings back-VAT liability that would otherwise not have applied.
  5. Incorrect invoice sequence — the VAT Act prescribes serial numbering; gaps or duplicates draw attention.
  6. Incorrect apportionment of input credit for mixed-supply businesses.
  7. Missing to charge VAT on advance receipts — VAT is due when payment is received or the invoice is issued, whichever is earlier.
  8. Not maintaining electronic backup — a lost register during audit is treated as concealment.

Each of these is cheap to prevent and expensive to fix after an assessment.

Penalties and interest — what late or wrong filing costs

Common VAT penalties (Nepal, FY 2082/83)

Bar length is directional, not to scale — concealment penalty is materially larger than the routine late-filing charges.

  • Late deposit interest: 15% per annum under Section 26 of the VAT Act.
  • Late filing fee: 0.05% of tax per day of default (with a floor and cap).
  • Non-issuance of tax invoice: flat penalty per instance, plus back tax.
  • Concealment / evasion: up to 100% of the tax shortfall in penalty in addition to the tax and interest.
  • Prolonged non-filing (more than six months): can trigger de-registration and blacklisting.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to file if my business had no sales this month?

Yes. Nil returns are compulsory. Missing them is one of the most common — and easiest to avoid — sources of penalties.

Can I claim input credit on the vehicle I bought for business?

Generally no on private-passenger vehicles — the VAT Act restricts credit on automobiles except for specified sectors (transport operators, rent-a-car). Commercial vehicles used solely in taxable business (delivery vans, freight trucks) qualify.

What if I discover an error in a return I've already filed?

You may file a revised return for the affected month before the assessment period closes. Voluntary correction generally attracts interest but not the punitive concealment penalty — which is why it's usually cheaper to correct proactively than to be caught.

How is the local house-rent tax different from VAT?

House-rent tax is a local (municipal) levy, typically 10%, collected by the local body — not VAT and not IRD. See our TDS guide for the full withholding picture.

Is e-filing mandatory in FY 2082/83?

Yes — for the vast majority of registered persons. Paper filing survives only for a narrow set of taxpayers where the IRD has explicitly permitted it. Most Kathmandu-valley businesses have been e-filing for several years.
