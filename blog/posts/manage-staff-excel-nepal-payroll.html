Manage Staff in Nepal Using Excel (No HR Software): Attendance, Leaves, Roster and Monthly Payroll

By Upaj Manandhar, Chartered Accountant Jul 17, 2026 16 min read

Most small Nepali businesses pay for HR or payroll software they only half-use — because "everyone else does." For the first 20–30 employees, a properly-designed Excel workbook covers everything the Labour Act 2074 and the Income Tax Act 2058 require: employee master, attendance, leave, holiday calendar, roster/duty planner, and monthly payroll with SSF, EPF, CIT, TDS and the 10% female rebate. This guide gives you the full workbook design — six sheets, the formulas that make them work, and the workflow that keeps them honest month after month.

Key takeaways

  • A six-sheet Excel workbook — Master, Attendance, Leave, Holidays, Roster, Payroll — replaces HR software for most SMEs.
  • Use one date column as the primary key. Every leave day, roster slot and pay-period link back through the same date.
  • Nepal's Labour Act 2074 sets the leave minima: ~18 days annual, 12 sick, 13 public holidays, 98 maternity.
  • Payroll must handle the FY 2083/84 unified slab (1% / 10% / 20% / 27% / 29%), SSF vs non-SSF employees, and the 10% female rebate.
  • Protect the workbook with sheet-level passwords and a change-log; keep the master file in a Shared Drive (see our Drive/OneDrive guide).

When Excel is enough (and when it isn't)

Excel handles staff management well up to a point. It struggles beyond it. Roughly:

  • Excel wins for 1–30 headcount, no biometric device, monthly manual attendance sign-off, one payroll cycle.
  • Excel starts to strain at 30–50 headcount, when self-service and mobile access matter.
  • Excel loses above 50 headcount, or when biometric/RFID attendance, shift-swap workflows, or SSO-based employee portals become the norm.

Even in an HR-software world, the Excel discipline of "one master, one date key, one formula per calculation" is exactly what makes payroll auditable. Learn it here first.

The six-sheet workbook design

Sheet-to-sheet data flow

Six sheets, one direction of data flow. Payroll is a computed output — never data-entered directly.

1. Employee Master

The Master sheet is the primary key of the whole workbook. One row per employee, never edited during the month — only additions and status changes.

Every other sheet uses Emp ID to look up back into Master via XLOOKUP. Never duplicate data.

2. Attendance

Design: rows are employees, columns are dates. Codes: P (Present), A (Absent unpaid), L (Leave), H (Holiday), W (Weekly off), WFH (Work from home). Data entered daily or captured from a punch export.

  • Colour-conditional formatting: green for P/WFH, yellow for L, red for A, grey for H/W.
  • Weekly summary column: =COUNTIF(range,"P")+COUNTIF(range,"WFH").
  • Present days for payroll: =COUNTIFS(range,"P")+COUNTIFS(range,"WFH")+COUNTIFS(range,"L") // paid days
  • Unpaid absences: =COUNTIF(range,"A"). This is what triggers a salary deduction.
  • Working days in the month: =NETWORKDAYS.INTL(FirstOfMonth, EndOfMonth, "0000011", HolidayRange) — the "0000011" pattern makes Saturday the weekly off (adjust if Friday-Saturday weekend is your policy).

3. Leave Register

One row per leave application. Columns:

  • Emp ID, Employee (auto), Type, From, To, Days, Reason, Approver, Status.
  • Type = Annual / Sick / Casual / Home / Maternity / Paternity / Bereavement / Unpaid.
  • Days: =NETWORKDAYS.INTL(From, To, "0000011", Holidays). Do not count holidays as leave.

Balance calculation (per employee, per type):

=Opening + Accrual − IF([Applied]="Approved",[Days],0)

  • Annual leave accrual: ~1.5 days/month (approximates the Labour Act's 1 day per 20 days worked; adjust to your policy).
  • Sick leave: 12 days a year, non-carry-forward or carry-forward capped — your policy choice.
  • Home leave / Casual: per your policy.
  • Maternity: 98 days paid, per Labour Act 2074.

4. Public Holidays

A simple two-column sheet: Date and Holiday Name. Update once a year against the Government of Nepal's official calendar.

Nepal's Labour Act 2074 recognises 13 public holidays plus each employee's weekly off. Common holidays include:

  • Dashain (typically 5 working days off around Vijaya Dashami)
  • Tihar (2–3 working days)
  • Chhath (in Tarai regions)
  • Loshar (community-specific — Tamu, Sonam, Gyalpo)
  • Eid (Fitr and Bakrid; community-specific)
  • National Day / Democracy Day / Republic Day / Constitution Day / Labour Day
  • New Year (Baisakh 1) and gender-specific holidays such as Rishi Panchami (women).

The Nepal Government publishes the year's exact holiday calendar every Chaitra/Baisakh. Load it into this sheet once a year — every other sheet will pick it up automatically.

5. Roster / Duty Planner

Roster is the forward-looking cousin of Attendance. Design: rows are dates, columns are shifts (or employees). Fill cells with the Emp ID assigned to that slot.

  • For a 2-shift office (Day / Evening): two columns of assignments per date, plus an On-call column.
  • Validation: Data → Data Validation → List from the Master Emp ID range, so every cell must resolve to a real employee.
  • Cross-check: =COUNTIF(RosterRow, EmpID) flags if a single employee is assigned twice on the same day.
  • Overtime slot flag: any assignment beyond 8 hours per day should be tagged OT for later payroll pickup.
  • Coverage check per date: =COUNTA(RosterRow) vs required headcount — highlights under-cover.

Publish the roster at least a week in advance. If your team includes shift-swap requests, capture them in a separate Swaps sheet with approver sign-off — do not overwrite the original roster.

6. Payroll — the monthly calculation

Payroll is a computed sheet, not a data-entry sheet. Every value comes from an upstream lookup or formula. One row per employee per month; add a Period column so a single sheet holds every month.

Slab formula — FY 2083/84 unified schedule

The FY 2083/84 slab (see our salary slab post): 1% first 10 lakh, 10% next 5 lakh, 20% next 10 lakh, 27% next 15 lakh, 29% above 40 lakh. Using LET for readability:

=LET(t, TaxableIncome,
    b1, MIN(t, 1000000),
    b2, MIN(MAX(t-1000000,0), 500000),
    b3, MIN(MAX(t-1500000,0), 1000000),
    b4, MIN(MAX(t-2500000,0), 1500000),
    b5, MAX(t-4000000,0),
    b1*IF(IsSSF,0,0.01) + b2*0.1 + b3*0.2 + b4*0.27 + b5*0.29
)

SSF contributors have the 1% Social Security Tax on the first band waived; non-SSF pay 1% on the first NPR 10 lakh.

Worked example — Sita's monthly payslip

Sita is a Marketing Executive at a Kathmandu SME. Female. Individual filing. Enrolled in SSF. FY 2083/84.

  • Basic: NPR 45,000/month
  • Allowances: NPR 15,000/month
  • Gross: NPR 60,000/month → annualised NPR 7,20,000
  • SSF (11%): NPR 4,950/month → annual NPR 59,400
  • Life insurance premium: NPR 25,000 per year
  • Attendance: 22 P + 4 W + 1 H = 27 days; 0 unpaid absence in a 26-working-day month → full month paid.

Annualised taxable income: 7,20,000 − 59,400 − 25,000 = NPR 6,35,600.

Annual tax (SSF contributor, so 0% on first 10 lakh):

  • 0% × 6,35,600 = NPR 0

Female rebate: 0 × 10% = 0.

Monthly TDS: NPR 0. Net take-home = 60,000 − 4,950 = NPR 55,050.

Under the old FY 2082/83 slab Sita would have paid ~NPR 8,500 a year — the FY 2083/84 unified 1% band up to 10 lakh eliminates it completely for SSF contributors below that ceiling.

Where the salary rupee goes — Sita's payslip

Sita's take-home under FY 2083/84 (SSF contributor). The unified 1% band up to NPR 10 lakh removes salary tax at this income level entirely.

Payroll is not "typing numbers into a sheet." Payroll is a calculation — every rupee traceable to a rule, every deduction to a statutory basis. Design it that way from day one.

Monthly workflow — 8 steps

  1. Day 1–25: Attendance sheet updated daily by a supervisor. Colour formatting flags problems in real time.
  2. Day 25: Freeze attendance. Any late corrections require a change-log entry.
  3. Day 26: Reconcile leaves — every L code in Attendance must have a corresponding row in Leave Register (and vice versa).
  4. Day 26: Pull OT from Roster / OT log. Verify against the 4-hour/day, 24-hour/week cap.
  5. Day 26: Add any variable earnings (project bonus, incentive) or deductions (advance recovery).
  6. Day 27: Compute payroll. Sanity-check: total gross vs previous month, headcount vs Master, TDS vs previous month.
  7. Day 27: Payslips generated (per-employee PDF export from a payslip template sheet). Approvals from HR head + Finance head.
  8. Day 28–30: Bank transfer file uploaded. SSF/EPF/CIT/TDS deposit challans prepared. TDS deposit due by 25th of the following Nepali month (Section 90, Income Tax Act).

Controls, security and audit trail

  • Workbook stored in a Shared drive. Version history is your undo. See our Drive / OneDrive guide.
  • Sheet protection: Payroll formulas locked; only variable-input cells unlocked. Password per sheet.
  • Change log sheet: Date, Who, What changed, Why. Every mid-month attendance correction lands here.
  • Two-person approval for payroll release. HR + Finance heads' initials on the paid-out sheet.
  • Backup snapshots: Save-as at month-end into an "Archive" folder. Never edit historical months.
  • PII handling: PAN and bank account are personal data — restrict edit access to HR + Finance; never share the workbook externally without redacting these columns.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need SSF, EPF or both?

The Social Security Fund (SSF) is the government's unified scheme covering retirement, medical, accident, dependent and maternity benefits, funded by 11% employee + 20% employer contribution on basic salary. Businesses that enrolled in SSF stopped contributing separately to EPF for those employees. Non-SSF businesses continue with EPF (10% + 10%) and typically add CIT as a voluntary retirement top-up.

Can I run bi-weekly or semi-monthly payroll from this workbook?

Yes. Split the Payroll sheet into two half-month cycles with the same schema; the annualised tax computation still runs on the full year, so TDS smoothing works.

How do I handle salary revisions mid-year?

Add an "Effective From" column on the Master sheet and use it as a date filter in the payroll lookup. Never overwrite the previous salary — history matters for statutory calculations and disputes.

What about final settlement on resignation?

Compute pro-rata leave encashment on unused annual leave; pay pending salary up to the last working day; issue TDS certificate (Form 25-Ka) within 15 days of fiscal-year-end. Provident/SSF withdrawal is handled by the fund separately.

Can this workbook produce a payslip PDF?

Yes — a dedicated Payslip template sheet with XLOOKUP-driven fields (Emp ID as the input), then File → Print → Save as PDF, or a small VBA/macro loop that saves one PDF per employee. For less than 30 staff this takes 3 minutes a month.

Getting your workbook built

Building this workbook for a 15-person business takes roughly a working day for someone who knows Excel well. If yours is a mid-sized practice with unusual pay structures — production incentive, sales commission, home-leave pay — the design has to be adjusted, but the six-sheet skeleton stays the same.
